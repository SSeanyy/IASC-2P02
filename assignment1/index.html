1. Flatness

From inside the cave...

During a brutal conflict between opposing parties, many men are captured and exiled to a cave in a land far from civilization. They are set to serve life alone in a dark and wretched cave. There, they sit in complete darkness as the constant pacing from the guard deafens them. Hours turn into weeks and weeks turn into years, and still the men remain helpless, barely clinging onto life. Prisoners held within this cave are completely banished from reality. The only glimpse of the real world prisoners have is the guard patrolling the entrance. When the sun rises, its rays shine bright upon the cave wall. It illuminates the shadow of the guard onto the wall, constantly pacing the entrance back and forth. It’s been years since the men exiled to this prison were served a proper meal. A rice cracker and a bowl of porridge served once daily are what keep these men barely clinging to life. They feel a sense of excitement whenever the guard approaches them with their meal, but still, they know their future remains here, forever trapped in this forsaken cave. These shadows cast onto the cave wall serve as the only perception the prisoners have of the reality.

Nick Sousanis explains that the concept of flatness is where an individual is essentially imprisoned within a two-dimensional space, limiting the individual's perspective of reality to what is only in front of them and not around them. In addition, the concept of flatness also limits the individual’s ability to properly understand and interpret what exactly the “real world” is. The prisoners within the cave embody this concept due to the fact that their only perception of reality is the cave wall and the shadows being cast onto the wall. The sun illuminating the cave wall ultimately serves as the bridge between a two-dimensional space and a three-dimensional space by turning shadows on the cave wall into their only perspective of reality. Their perception of the real world relies heavily on the shadows being illuminated onto the cave wall. Without such, they would fail to recognize and interpret what reality actually is, apart from the cave itself. The prisoners within the cave are completely accustomed only to what’s in front of them, rather than what lies beyond the cave, leaving them to truly believe that what they see on the cave wall is the real world.

2. Seeing Double

From outside the cave...

From outside the cave, there stands a guard patrolling the cave entrance, preventing the prisoners from escaping. Helpless prisoners trapped within a cave, limiting their interactions and their perception of what actually the outside world really is. As the sun rises and the day begins, there are skies of blue and clouds of white. The sun shines bright upon the deserted land, lighting up inside the cave wall with the guard’s shadow, creating the only connection and perception the prisoners have with reality. But when the sun slowly starts to set, the silhouette of the guard begins fading. As night falls, the environment surrounding the guard turns to complete darkness, and it seems like the sun is gradually disappearing from the face of the Earth. During this feeling of impending doom, it seems like the bridge between the two and three-dimensional spaces starts to collapse, but even in the complete darkness, the Moon awakes. The glare barely lights the inside of the cave, but still manages to cling to that bridge between the dimensional spaces. Being outside the cave allows individuals to properly interpret and perceive reality. In a three-dimensional space, you can interpret and understand the different layers of reality itself and recognize that several elements come and work together to create what an individual perceives outside the cave. From inside the cave, there is depth or elements, only the shadows being cast onto the cave wall, forming a lack of understanding of what the outside world truly is.

The concept of Seeing Double according to Nick Sousanis is described as a bridge between two perspectives at the same time, and how there is no “correct” view. In this case, two perspectives occur at the same time, one from inside the cave and one from outside the cave. From inside the cave, the Sun illuminates the inside, casting a shadow silhouette of the guard onto the cave wall, essentially creating the prisoner’s only perception of reality. From outside the cave, you recognize there are hidden layers and elements that form the environment around us, rather than just a shadow. From inside the cave, it may look like the Sun and guard are fading away from existence as they disappear into complete darkness, but in reality, the Sun is just gradually setting, making it harder for light to project the guard’s shadow against the cave wall. But an individual inside the cave would not be able to fully grasp the concept of three-dimensional spaces due to the fact that their only perception of reality itself is what they see in front of them, rather than what is around them.
